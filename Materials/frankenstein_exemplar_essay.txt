Mary Shelley's Frankenstein critiques the pursuit of scientific knowledge without ethical responsibility, arguing that the danger of Victor Frankenstein's ambition lies not in the act of creation itself but in his refusal to accept responsibility for what he creates. Victor's tragedy is not that he oversteps the boundaries of science, but that he treats discovery as an end in itself, divorced from any duty of care toward its consequences.

The clearest evidence of this failure comes immediately after the creature awakens. Rather than caring for the being he has just brought to life, Victor flees in horror and abandons him, a decision that sets every subsequent tragedy in motion. Shelley lingers on this moment deliberately: Victor's revulsion is not directed at some abstract danger, but at the literal newborn creature standing before him, helpless and alone. The abandonment is immediate and total, and it is this rejection, more than the act of creation, that the novel returns to again and again as the true source of harm.

This abandonment is not incidental to the novel's argument but central to it. The creature's later violence emerges directly from the isolation and rejection Victor imposes on him. Left to learn the world alone, denied companionship, and repeatedly driven away by the very people he tries to approach with kindness, the creature is shaped by cruelty long before he commits any cruelty of his own. Shelley is careful to show this development in the creature's own words, so that his turn toward violence reads not as an inevitable feature of his nature, but as the outcome of Victor's continued refusal to take responsibility for him.

One might argue that the creature bears responsibility for his own crimes regardless of how he was treated, since he ultimately chooses to kill innocent people, including William, Justine, and Clerval. This objection has force: Shelley does not excuse the creature's actions, and the novel does not ask readers to simply forgive them. But by giving the creature a long, articulate account of his own development, Shelley complicates any reading that treats him as simply monstrous. He is shown to be capable of compassion, curiosity, and longing for connection, qualities that are answered at every turn with fear and violence from others. The novel's argument is not that Victor's failure erases the creature's guilt, but that it explains and precedes it, locating the deeper ethical failure in Victor's refusal to accept responsibility rather than in the creature's nature alone.

Read this way, Frankenstein is less a warning against scientific ambition itself than a warning against ambition unaccompanied by responsibility. Victor's true crime is not that he created life, but that having done so, he refused to remain its author.
